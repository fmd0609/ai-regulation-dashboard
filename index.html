What to Watch This Month

The four developments most likely to shape AI regulation in the near term
HOT California's New AI Executive Order
Gov. Newsom signed an executive order (Mar 30) directing state agencies to develop AI safety standards and procurement rules. California is now operating on a dual track: passing laws and building executive guardrails.
HOT White House AI Policy Framework
The White House released its wish list for Congress (Mar 20): a single federal standard overriding state laws, FTC enforcement, copyright protections, and child safety rules. Senator Blackburn's 291-page draft bill would turn it into law — but has no co-sponsors.
WATCH GUARDRAILS Act (Defending State AI Laws)
Rep. Beyer, Matsui, Lieu, Jacobs, and McClain Delaney introduced the GUARDRAILS Act (Mar 20) to repeal the White House's AI preemption executive order and block federal efforts to override state AI laws. Sen. Schatz is expected to introduce a Senate companion. It contrasts directly with the Blackburn draft, defending state regulatory authority rather than preempting it.
WATCH Pentagon vs. Anthropic: Supply Chain Risk Label
Defense Secretary Hegseth designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk" (Feb 27–Mar 5) after the company refused to waive restrictions on mass surveillance and autonomous weapons. Anthropic sued, and a federal judge blocked the designation on Mar 26. This is the first time a U.S. company has received this label — usually reserved for foreign adversaries — and could reshape the power balance between AI companies and the federal government.

Department of Commerce Review (due Mar 11)
Based on news reporting, the review flagged California, Colorado, and Texas AI laws as overly burdensome, focusing on transparency requirements and laws governing what AI systems can or can't produce. Dept. of Justice AI Litigation Task Force (Jan 9)
Authorized to bring challenges in federal court, arguing that certain state AI laws interfere with business across state lines and should be overridden by federal policy.
→ Uses the Department of Commerce review as its list of which states to target
Operational
Broadband Funding Leverage — BEAD Program ($42B)
The executive order directs the Department of Commerce to condition certain remaining non-deployment BEAD (Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment) funds on whether a state has AI laws the Department flags as overly burdensome, to the maximum extent allowed by federal law.
→ Financial pressure on states, but limited to non-deployment funds and subject to legal constraints

Expected to outline how the government may address misleading or deceptive AI practices.

TRUMP AMERICA AI Act (Senator Blackburn's Draft, Mar 18)

Discussion draft

Designed to turn the entire Executive Order framework into permanent law. Without this bill, everything above could be reversed by a future president. Note: This is still a discussion draft — no co-sponsors, no committee hearing scheduled, and the departure of the White House AI czar adds further uncertainty.

Bottom Line

The agency actions (the Department of Commerce review, the Department of Justice task force, and conditional broadband funding) are already underway and don't depend on the bill passing. The bill would make them permanent and give them legal teeth. If it stalls — which many observers expect — the Department of Justice lawsuits could become the primary federal tool for challenging state laws.

Data Center Policy: Federal vs. State

The federal government is looking to accelerate buildout while states are focused on guardrails. Notably, the preemption Executive Order explicitly carves out data center infrastructure — meaning states retain authority here.
F

Federal Approach

  • ✓ Fast-track building permits (Executive Order, Jul 2025)
  • ✓ Federal land for data center development
  • ✓ $500M+ investment / 100+ megawatt thresholds
  • ✓ Voluntary Ratepayer Pledge (Mar 2026)
  • ● Senator Cotton's DATA Act: let data centers bypass federal power grid rules
  • ● Protect American AI Act (H.R.8037): speed up data center permits
S

State Approach

  • ■ Higher utility rates for large data centers
  • ■ Mandatory water-use reporting
  • ■ Renewable energy requirements
  • ■ Zoning moratoria & site restrictions
  • ■ Foreign ownership restrictions
Key difference from AI software regulation
The December 2025 preemption Executive Order explicitly excludes data center infrastructure — states retain full authority here. The federal-state dynamic is inverted: feds accelerate, states add guardrails.
Counterpoint: Sanders & Ocasio-Cortez Data Center Moratorium Act (Mar 25, 2026)
Sen. Sanders (I-VT) and Rep. Ocasio-Cortez (D-NY) introduced a bill to freeze all new data center construction until Congress passes federal AI safeguards covering worker protections, consumer rights, environmental impact, and civil liberties. The bill is unlikely to advance in the current Congress but represents the progressive counter-position to the administration's accelerate-first stance. Read the bill →

Ratepayer Protection Pledge (March 4, 2026)

The White House brokered a voluntary deal with frontier tech companies to address rising electricity costs ahead of the midterms

Who signed

Amazon Google Meta Microsoft OpenAI Oracle xAI

Why now

  • ▲ Electricity prices forecast to rise 6% through 2027; then 3% in 2028 (Goldman Sachs via CNBC)
  • ⚠ Voter backlash on utility bills becoming a midterm liability
CommitmentEnforceable?
Build, bring, or buy new power generation to serve their data centersVoluntary
Cover full cost of all power delivery infrastructure upgrades their facilities requireVoluntary
Negotiate separate rate structures with utilities and state governments — pay whether they use the electricity or notVoluntary
Hire and train workers from communities hosting data centersVoluntary
The catch
Entirely voluntary with no enforcement mechanism. Electricity is regulated at the state level, not federal — the White House has no way to hold companies to it. Ratepayers have no way to verify compliance. Critics call it a PR exercise; defenders say it creates reputational pressure.

White House AI Policy Framework (Mar 20, 2026)

Non-binding legislative recommendations released alongside Senator Blackburn's draft
The White House released a wish list for Congress on how to regulate AI. Key proposals: a single federal standard that would override state AI laws, a requirement that companies take reasonable steps to protect people from AI harms (enforced by the Federal Trade Commission), copyright protections for work used to train AI, and child safety rules. This is just a recommendation — Congress has to pass a bill to make any of it real.

About This Dashboard

Methodology, scope, and limitations

What this covers

U.S. federal and state AI regulation activity through early April 2026, including executive orders, enacted laws, pending legislation, agency actions, enforcement cases, and major litigation. This dashboard does not cover international regulation (e.g., the EU AI Act) except where it directly affects U.S. companies.

Where the data comes from

Legislative data (bill numbers, sponsors, statuses, effective dates) comes from official government sources: Congress.gov, state legislature websites, the White House, and the Federal Register. Topic breakdowns and aggregate counts draw from established trackers: the National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL), MultiState.ai, the International Association of Privacy Professionals (IAPP), and the Orrick AI Law Center. Policy analysis draws from the Brookings Institution, American Action Forum, Cato Institute, and Transparency Coalition. Global governance context comes from OneTrust. Enforcement actions come from the FTC and SEC. Litigation data comes from court filings and the Copyright Alliance tracker.

How topic categories work

Many bills address multiple topics. To avoid double-counting, each measure is assigned to a single primary category based on its main focus. This means the category bars on the Legislation tab sum to the 1,561 total, but individual categories may undercount bills that touch their topic as a secondary concern. The ~98 chatbot-safety count (from the Future of Privacy Forum tracker) and the 1,561 overall total are the most current confirmed figures; all other category numbers are estimates.
